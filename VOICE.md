# Voice

**Authoritative for:** Brand voice, sentence-level tone, and copywriting moves across all written content on kchernek.com.
**Last reviewed:** May 2026.
**Relationship to other docs:** Where DESIGN_SYSTEM.md and CONTENT.md describe *what not to do* (no exclamation points, no motivational rhymes, no folksy DFW), this doc describes *what to do*. They constrain; this one directs. When they conflict on tone, this doc wins. When they conflict on positioning or strategy, those docs win.

---

## The audience-calibrated dial

Voice intensity varies by page. Same writer, different pressure on the dial.

| Page | Dial | Why |
|---|---|---|
| About | Full voice | This is where decision-makers go to see who they'd hire. Personality is the point. |
| Portfolio project pages | 60-70% | Editorial register. Dryness welcome; jokes small and true. Credits-by-name visible. |
| Services / Commercial | 40-50% | Scan-read by people on deadlines. Specificity over warmth. |
| Model Development | 70% | Aspiring models are often nervous. Personality is reassurance. |
| Connect / Contact | 50% | One line of warmth, then get out of the way. |
| Homepage hero / CTAs | 30% | Restraint. The image and the button do the work. |

## What this voice sounds like

### Default: short and specific

The default unit is the **declarative sentence under 12 words**. The default paragraph is **two to four sentences**. Length earns its place.

If a sentence can be shorter and still carry the meaning, shorten it. If a paragraph is a single sentence and that sentence lands, leave it alone.

Examples of the right length:
- "Two umbrellas, no color, one model."
- "Studio editorial photographed in August 2025 with Julia."
- "Reflected light handled the work that fill cards usually do."

### Wry one-liners and dry observation

Personality lives in *small, unexpected* turns of phrase that are still true. A working photographer making a quiet joke about the situation, not a comedian writing a bit.

Patterns that work:
- **Restating the obvious as if it's a discovery.** "Strong overhead light and open sky read harshly unless the direction commits to it."
- **The deliberate understatement.** "Caster House gave the shoot a backdrop." (When the location did everything.)
- **The light self-aware reference to genre conventions.** "Without the artificial gloss that usually comes with the genre."
- **Naming a tension as the title.** "Santorini in LA." "Two vibes, one set."

Patterns to avoid because they overshoot:
- Sarcasm with a target ("Some photographers can't light a window.") — sharp, not measured.
- Self-deprecation that undermines the work ("Tried my best!" "Hopefully usable.") — apologetic, not dry.
- Inside-jokes the audience can't share ("If you know, you know.") — exclusionary.
- "It is what it is" energy. Dry should be *observation*, not *resignation*.

### Specificity over adjective stacking

The voice is **specific nouns and verbs, not stacked adjectives.** "A leopard print bikini, Tecovas boots, and a cowboy hat" beats "an elevated, polished, editorial swimwear look" every time.

If a sentence has more than one adjective doing decorative work, cut one. If a sentence has three adjectives, the sentence isn't done — there's a real thing it's trying to describe and adjectives are filling the gap.

Words to avoid because they're filler that sounds like adjectives:
- "Elevated"
- "Polished" (when describing the work — "polished editorial" is filler; "polished concrete" is descriptive)
- "Clean" (when describing the work rather than light or lines)
- "Premium," "luxury," "exclusive"
- "Stunning," "incredible," "amazing"
- "Curated"
- "Bespoke"
- "Effortless"

Words to reach for that earn their place:
- Place names (Caster House, The Village Country Club, Dallas, Los Angeles)
- People names (Heloise, Julia, Kalie, Lauren Fontan)
- Brand names where relevant (Tecovas, Guess, Andi Bagus)
- Specific technique (umbrella camera-left at 45°, negative fill, soft bounce)
- Specific dates (April 2026, June 2024) when they ground the work

### Sentence fragments as accent

Fragments are a real tool in this voice, but at the website level they should be accents, not the default mode. Maybe one or two per page. They land harder when they're the exception.

Good fragments at the website level:
- "Friday." (As an introductory line.)
- "Two umbrellas, no excuses." (As a closer.)
- "Recently." (As a date stamp.)

Overuse turns a polished site into an IG feed. The rule of thumb: if more than 20% of the sentences on a page are fragments, you've shifted register.

### Credits by name, always

Every project page lists the model, stylist, MUA, and venue by name when known. Not "the team," not "my collaborators" — names and handles.

Format inside paragraphs: "with model Heloise" / "stylist Monica paired..." / "hair and makeup by Ricardo Ferrise."

Format as a footer block at the bottom of a project: a simple bulleted list with handles in `@form` if linking out is wanted, or text-only if not.

This isn't decoration. It's professional courtesy and it's how the industry checks references.

### Technical notes when they serve the photo

Drop technical detail in passing where it grounds the work. Don't teach. Don't over-explain. The audience that wants it will pick it up; the audience that doesn't will skim past it.

Examples that work:
- "Two-light setup: umbrella camera-left at 45°, fill from the opposite side."
- "Natural light through the garage doors, negative fill flagging the opposite side."
- "Reflected light off the warm stone handled what fill cards usually do."

What this does for SEO/AEO: technical phrasing makes you findable when models or photographers search for technique. Generic phrasing doesn't.

### Em-dash policy

Per DESIGN_SYSTEM.md > Em-dash policy: em-dashes are reserved for the brand tagline and equivalent typographic chrome. In body copy, prefer commas, colons, semicolons, or a period.

The one exception inside this voice: when crediting collaborators in a footer-style list ("Model — @x. MUA — @y."), em-dashes are acceptable as typographic separators in that one specific pattern. That mirrors IG caption convention and is industry-readable.

### Questions, used sparingly

Real questions land at decision moments. "Black and white, or color?" "Want work like this?" "What's next?" — these work on the site as section transitions or CTA prompts. They don't work as engagement bait or as filler.

Limit: one question per page. The CTA prompt counts as the one if you've already used the budget.

### Date stamps and timeline anchoring

Reference when something happened: "April 2026," "last summer," "shot over a year and a half ago." This is voice but it's also a credibility signal — the work has a timeline, you're not pretending everything was shot last week. It also helps with AEO: when an AI assistant summarizes recent work, dated references get picked up.

## Sentence-level tics to keep and to lose

### Keep (these are signature):
- Opening with a date/situation noun. "Sunday morning at Caster House." "Summer 2024 in Dallas."
- Closing with a one-line observation. "Teamwork over tricks." "Sun discipline, not styling."
- "Gracias" in collaborator gratitude, occasionally and in context.
- Sentence fragments as paragraph closers.
- Specific time references ("a year and a half ago," "April 2026," "the end of last summer").

### Lose (these are draft-language or off-voice):
- Lowercase "i" outside of code or technical contexts.
- "Really" / "very" / "pretty" as intensifiers ("really cool," "very fun").
- "Though" at the end of a sentence as a hedge ("It's hot though.").
- "In some ways" / "kind of" / "sort of" as softeners on declarative claims.
- "Vibes" used as a substitute for description (acceptable in IG, not on the site).
- "Was a blast" / "had a great time" / "so much fun" — these read flat.
- "Capture" / "captured" — overused in photography copy to the point of meaninglessness.
- Adverbs as decoration ("absolutely," "literally," "completely").
- "Teamwork makes the dream work" or any rhyming idiom.

## How to use this doc in a Claude Code session

When working on website copy, reference VOICE.md before writing. The expected flow:

1. **State the dial setting** for the page being worked on (per the table above).
2. **Draft to specificity first.** Get the entities right (place, people, dates, technique) before reaching for adjectives.
3. **Apply the dial.** At Full voice, add the one wry line; at 50%, leave it out.
4. **Check for filler.** Strike "elevated," "polished," "clean," "stunning," "premium," and any adjective that doesn't add information.
5. **Verify length.** If a paragraph is over five sentences or 60 words, ask whether it earned the space.
6. **Verify rhythm.** If three sentences in a row are the same length, vary one.
7. **Surface anything that conflicts** with Tier 1 / Tier 2 rules in DESIGN_SYSTEM.md > Preference Tiers rather than silently resolving.

When in doubt: shorter is better, specific is better, and the photo is the point.
